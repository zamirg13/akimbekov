Blog Post 1: Introduction to McKinsey-like Problem Solving

Imagine you're a detective, trying to solve a mystery. That's what problem solving is like. Just like a detective, we follow a process. McKinsey-like problem solving has seven steps:

  1. Problem Definition: Like understanding what mystery we're trying to solve. Let's say our bicycle is broken.
  2. Problem Structuring: We divide our main problem into smaller, easier to handle problems. Like figuring out if the issue with the bicycle is with the chain, the tires, or the brakes.
  3. Prioritization: We focus on the most important smaller problems first. Maybe the tires are flat, so we decide to fix them before anything else.
  4. Issue Analysis: We dig deeper into our priority problems. Why are the tires flat? Did they get punctured?
  5. Synthesis: We bring together all the information we've collected. We've learned the tires are flat because they were punctured by a nail.
  6. Storylining: We create a story out of our findings. "The bicycle was not working because the tires were punctured by a nail."
  7. Communication: We share our story and solution with others. "I found a nail punctured our bicycle's tires, so I fixed them."

Just like a detective solves a mystery, we too can solve any problem following these steps. In our upcoming blogs, we will delve deeper into each step and learn how to apply them in our day-to-day life.
